New York City is turning six peaker power plants into renewables.
Here's what that means for the state.


By: Audrey Carleton
Date: November 16, 2020

Eddie Bautista, executive director of the New York City Environmental Justice Alliance, has spent years trying to move the city away from its reliance on peaking power plants.

Better known as ‘peaker plants,’ these fossil fuel power plants are only run a few times each year, when there’s peak demand from the city’s grid, like during extreme weather events. Despite their infrequent use, peaker plants tend to emit health-damaging pollutants like carbon dioxide and particulate matter at higher rates per unit of energy generated than traditional power plants. There are currently 16 across the city, and, according to Bautista, all are primarily located in low-income communities of color.

“On the hottest days of the year, the most polluting power plants get turned on, and they are in the communities with worse, already, air quality or public health profiles,” Bautista says. “Whether it's COVID, whether it's climate change, pick the indicator: People of color will tend to have worse outcomes.”

He’s currently leading a group of five environmental justice organizations, known together as the PEAK Coalition, which recently announced plans to work with the New York Power Authority (NYPA) to transition six of the city’s peaker plants into renewable energy stations for solar, wind or battery power. Many of these plants were built before or around 2000, and according to Bautista, were passed without a complete environmental impact review using technology that is now dangerously out of date.

According to an original analysis of 2018 data on power plant emissions from the Environmental Protection Agency’s (EPA) Emissions & Generation Resource Integrated Database (EGRID), low-income counties across New York are facing higher emissions burdens from power plants of all kinds – peaker and otherwise – across the state. I linked 2018-19 income data from the U.S. Department of Agriculture’s to EGRID emissions data and found that regions where the median income is lower than the state average are 16 per cent more likely to be home to plants that emit higher-than-average annual levels of methane, a fossil fuel known to be up to 86 times more potent than carbon dioxide in its warming effect on the atmosphere.

An analysis of recorded emissions from 139 plants across the state also shows a correlation between income and exposure to methane from power plants; counties where median household income falls above the state average are, on the whole, exposed to lower levels of methane than those where the median household income is higher than the state average.

Here's how the numbers on fossil fuel power plant emissions in New York shake out.

26-million

The total recorded number of tons of carbon dioxide that the state of New York's fossil-fuel power plants emit per year. These 139 plants also relase 1.2-million pounds of methane and 2,219 tons of particulate matter 2.5 (PM2.5) – particles less than 2.5 micrometers in diameter that can penetrate deeply in the lungs and impair breathing – each year.








2.8-million

The total number of tons of carbon dioxide that the Astoria Generating Station emits each year. Located in the northwestern tip of the borough, the plant was the highest-recorded emitting plant in the state in 2018, and recently came under fire when parent utility, NRG Energy, proposed replacing its oil-burning infrastructure with gas-burning equivalents. “This proposal, just as any further expansion of fossil fuel infrastructure, is incompatible with our climate goals,” said city comptroller Scott Stringer in September.












16%

The increase in likelihood that a New Yorker will be exposed to higher-than-average amounts of methane from power plant emissions each year if they live in a low-income county compared to a high-income county. On the whole, New York's higher-emitting fossil fuel infrastructure is located in vulnerable communities; the city's peaker plants are clustered in low income pockets of Brooklyn, the Bronx, and Queens, that are already facing high cumulative burdens from existing industrial activity. "[Communities in the] Bronx do have a really high cumulative burden of environmental pollution and the peakers are one part of that," says Elena Krieger, director of research at PSE Healthy Energy.












While Krieger says peakers are only one part of the city's and state's pollution story, she believes starting a just transition in the state's most burdened environmental justice communities is the best way for NYPA to go. The Bronx tops the list of places to start.

A geospatial analysis of carbon dioxide, methane, and PM2.5 emissions across the state of New York show that the majority of counties with the highest total annual emissions levels are upstate – with Albany and Allegany topping the list. While 9 of the top ten counties by emissions are upstate, the Bronx is the only downstate county to make this list.



For Bautista, transitioning even a handful of peaker plants toward renewables, like solar and wind, represents the culmination of a 20-year fight – and he sees this as a central step in achieving New York's climate goals. "Other fights and other issues have come up over the years," he says. "But it's always been something that has been in the back of our mind."

Methodology:

This graphic and analysis were created using data from the Environmental Protection Agency's (EPA) 2018 Emissions & Generation Resource Integrated Database (EGRID). This data was linked to five-year (2014-2018) U.S. census data from Census Reporter, 2018 EGRID data on particulate matter 2.5 (PM2.5), and 2018-19 Unemployment and Median Household Income data from the U.S. Department of Agriculture's Economic Research Service. This set was analyzed for descriptive statistics (proportions and percentages) and odds ratios, used to determine the probability of higher-emitting plants being located in lower-income regions.
